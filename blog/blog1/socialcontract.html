Revisiting Rousseau's Social Contract in Light of Technological Advancements

If Jean-Jacques Rousseau, the 18th century philosopher who is known for his contributions to political philosophy and the concept of the social contract, were alive today, he would likely have a complex view of recent developments in modern technology landscape.

On one hand he might have believed AI as manifestation of human power and greater control over environment.

But, on the other hand:

He might have also expressed concerns about the potential negative consequences of AI on human society, such as job displacement, social inequality, and loss of privacy.

And I really wonder how might the new social contract in the age of AI might look like!

If you have read Rousseau, he was a firm believer of the fact that desire to pursuade others, having control and power over others was most basic human tendency. Thus, the social contract theory as a way to limit these impulses and tendencies in favour of more stable and egalitarian society.[1]

What is social contract theory?

Social contract theory proposes that individuals surrender some of their individual freedoms and rights to a governing body in exchange for protection and the maintenance of social order.

For a moment, think of a race, it is true that one person is always ahead of the other. In the same way, we can think of the relationship between Artificial Intelligence (AI), the Government, and the public interest. AI technology is rapidly advancing and leading the way in innovation and development. The government, on the other hand, is tasked with regulating the use of AI to ensure it aligns with public interest and ethical standards. However, there is often a gap between the pace of technological advancement and regulatory measures.

While AI is advancing quickly, government regulations struggle to keep up with the pace. Ultimately, the public interest is compromised at some point. Achieving the balance between these three elements is an ongoing challenge.

In ancient Greece, there was a wise man named Procrustes who owned an iron bed. Procrustes would invite weary travellers to spend the night in his home, offering them a place to rest on his bed. However, there was a catch: if the traveller was too short for the bed, Procrustes would stretch them until they fit. And if the traveller was too tall for the bed, Procrustes would chop off their limbs until they fit. Procrustes believed that his bed was perfect and that everyone should conform to its size.
